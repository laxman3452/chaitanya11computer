Computer Systems Study Guide

Short, High-Yield Notes for Quick Exam Revision

1. Hybrid Computer

Definition: A computer that combines the best features of both Analog and Digital computers.

  • Processes both continuous (analog) and discrete (digital) data.
  • Converts signals back and forth (Analog ↔ Digital).
  • Key Applications: Hospitals (heartbeat monitoring), Aeroplanes, Jet planes, and Industries.
  • Drawback: High cost (expensive).

Classification of Computers (By Size)

From largest/fastest to smallest/slowest:

  1. Supercomputer (Largest & Fastest)
  2. Mainframe Computer
  3. Mini Computer (Mid-range)
  4. Microcomputer (Smallest / Personal Devices)

2. Supercomputer

Core Features: Largest, fastest, and most expensive. Multi-user, multiprocessor systems utilizing parallel processing (multiple CPUs processing tasks at once).

  • Uses Multiple RISC (Reduced Instruction Set Computer) processors.
  • Historical Specs (Textbook): 64-bit systems, 100 MIPS speed, 8 MB storage.
  • Examples: CRAY-XMP/14, CDC-205, CYBER 205, ETA 10.

Top Exam Applications:

  • Petroleum Analyzing seismic data for oil exploration.
  • Aerospace Simulating airflow around aircraft (Aerodynamic designs).
  • Automobile Crash simulation designs before manufacturing.
  • Meteorology Weather forecasting.
  • Military Weapons research, defense systems, and decrypting sensitive intel.
  • Science DNA structure study and biomedical research (e.g., virus analysis).

3. Mainframe Computer

Core Features: Large, highly powerful general-purpose computers designed for large-scale data processing. Known as the "Enterprise Server".

  • Acts as the central "heart" of big businesses.
  • Users access it via Terminals (Screen + Keyboard, but no internal processing unit). Supports ~100 terminals simultaneously.
  • Can run multiple operating systems simultaneously (Virtual Machines).
  • Examples: IBM 1401, ICL 2959/10, IBM S/390, UNIVAC I (used by Census Bureau).
  • Applications: Banks, Airlines, Insurance companies, Railways, Government census data.

4. Mini Computer (Mid-range Systems)

Core Features: Medium-sized computers. Bigger than microcomputers, but smaller than mainframes. Developed in the 1960s using transistor and core memory technologies.

  • Supports a small number of terminals (approx. 50).
  • Less powerful/expensive than Mainframes; more powerful/expensive than Microcomputers.
  • Examples: IBM AS, Prime Series, HP 9000, PDP 11, IBM 8000 series.
  • Applications: Used by medium-sized firms or individual departments of large companies as centralized local servers.

5. Microcomputers (Personal Computers)

Core Features: The smallest and cheapest type of computer. Uses a Microchip / Microprocessor as its CPU. Designed for single-user use (1 person at a time).

  • Supports modern features: High-level programming languages, multimedia, 3D graphics, networking, GUI, and CUI.
  • Companies: IBM, Apple, Hewlett Packard (HP), Compaq.

Types of Microcomputers:

  • Desktop: Non-mobile workstation computer intended for a fixed location.
  • Laptop (Notebook): Light, compact, portable PC with a built-in battery, LCD monitor, and trackpad. Uses a clam-shell case design.
  • Handheld (Palmtop): Small enough to fit in your palm. Less powerful, runs specialized OS (like older Windows Mobile or Palm OS). Now mostly replaced by smartphones.
  • PDA (Personal Digital Assistant): Similar to palmtops but typically has no keyboard—uses a touch screen and a stylus with handwriting recognition. Used as a digital diary (e-mails, calendar, documents).
